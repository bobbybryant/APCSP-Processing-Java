Changing of the Seasons

Functions, arguments & conditionals — a guide for thinking through your scene before (and while) you build it.

This isn't the answer key. It's scaffolding. Poke at the interactive pieces below to get the concepts firmly in your head, then go invent a world that's entirely yours. The creativity is the part nobody can hand you.
01

The big idea

Strip away the shapes and colors, and this whole project is one simple loop of logic. Your scene is just facts about a world, handed to little helpers, who decide what to draw. Change the facts → the world changes.

Follow the value

The trickiest idea to feel is how a value travels. You declare it in one place, but it does its work somewhere else entirely. Change the season and the time below, and watch the same two values flow into drawSky (straight from your starter) and light up the branch that runs.

if (season == "SUMMER")summer sky

Notice the parameter is also named season — but it doesn't have to be. What flows in is the value, not the name. Try renaming it in your head to whichSeason — would anything break?

04

Think in ranges, not 24 cases

There are 24 possible hours, but you do not need 24 different looks. The trick is grouping hours into a handful of ranges — and that's exactly what a clean if / else if ladder does. Scrub through the day and watch which range each hour lands in.

These five bands come from your starter file. But your world doesn't have to keep the same hours. Maybe your dawn lingers, or "night" begins at 9. Where will your boundaries fall — and how many ranges do you actually need?

05

One job per function

The rubric rewards helpers that have a clear name, take the arguments they need, and do a single focused task. Big functions call smaller ones, which call smaller ones still. Here's the decomposition already living in your starter — click any function to see its one job.

  • setup()

    • drawLandscape(season, timeOfDay)
      • drawTree()
      • FruitfulLeaves(x, y)
      • drawPicnic(x, y, size)
      • drawSnowman(size)
      • Drawleaf()
        • drawOneLeaf(x, y)
← Click a function
Each box in the tree does exactly one thing. The bigger ones don't draw — they delegate, calling smaller helpers in the right order. That's the whole skill.
A genuinely useful nitpick: look closely at the names — the starter mixes styles like DrawCloud, drawTree, drawONEcloud, and Drawleaf. Capital-first? Lowercase-first? ALLCAPS in the middle? Pick one convention (camelCase — drawOneCloud — is the standard) and apply it everywhere. Consistent naming is exactly what graders mean by "clear names."
06

Plan your easter eggs

Easter eggs are things that appear only under specific conditions — snowflakes in winter, a moon at night. Each one is just a small drawing tucked inside a conditional. Tap any square to get a spark for that exact season + time combo (then aim to out-clever it).

Tap a square above to start brainstorming what only appears there.

The rubric gives the most points for easter eggs that are clever and appear only when appropriate. So for each idea, ask: which if statement makes this show up — and stay hidden the rest of the time?

Pre-flight checklist

A last sweep before you call it done. Tick each one as you confirm it.

Before you start — the non-negotiables
1. Create & SAVE your sketch first thing
2. Variables live locally in draw()/setup() — never global
3. Only variable assignments & function calls go in draw()/setup()
4. Format with ⌘ + T before submitting
5. Discuss ideas freely — but build on your own computer
