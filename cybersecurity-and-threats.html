01Where the cracks are

Why Attacks Work

Almost every attack targets one of two surfaces: the people using a system, or the machines and network running it. Strong technology can be undone by one tricked human, and a careful human can still be hit through a flaw in the wires or the code. Knowing which surface an attack aims at tells you which defense actually helps.

Attacking the human

Trick a person into giving up information or access — no need to break any encryption. This is social engineering, and phishing is its most common form. The fix is mostly awareness, not better tech.

Attacking the machine & network

Exploit software, intercept traffic, or overwhelm a server. Malware, man-in-the-middle, and denial-of-service attacks live here. The fixes are technical: encryption, software updates, redundancy, filtering.

02Bait & hook

Phishing

Phishing is a fake message — usually email or text — built to look like it’s from someone you trust, so you click a link, open an attachment, or hand over a password. The attacker isn’t breaking any code; they’re tricking you. The classic move is a message that manufactures urgency and then sends you to a page asking you to “confirm” personal information.

spot the difference (this is on the quiz)

A phishing link typically opens a fake web page that asks for your personal information (login, card number) — that’s the tell. Quietly recording your keystrokes is a different attack: a keylogger, a kind of malware (Section 04). Phishing tricks you into typing into the wrong place; a keylogger captures what you type anywhere.

Red-flag hunt

Here’s a suspicious email. Click each part that looks like a warning sign — there are five.

From: “School IT Help Desk” <it-helpdesk@school-secure-login.net>
Subject: URGENT: your account will be deleted in 24 hours

Dear valued user,

We detected a problem with your student account. To avoid permanent deletion, you must verify your identity immediately by clicking here: http://school-login-verify.net/account and confirming your username and password.

If you do not act within 24 hours, you will lose all of your files forever.

Thank you,
The IT Team

found: 0 / 5
Look-alike sender domain. A real school wouldn’t email from school-secure-login.net. Attackers register domains that resemble the real one.
Manufactured urgency. “24 hours or else” is designed to make you act before you think. Real notices rarely threaten instant deletion.
Generic greeting. “Dear valued user” means they don’t actually know who you are — a mass-sent fake.
Mismatched, non-HTTPS link. The link domain doesn’t match the school and uses plain http. Hover before clicking; better yet, type the real address yourself.
Threat + request for credentials. Pressure plus “confirm your username and password” is the heart of phishing. Legitimate IT never asks for your password.
defenses

Slow down on urgent messages; check the real sender address; never type a password into a page you reached by clicking an email link — navigate there yourself. And switch on the next section’s best defense: multi-factor authentication, so a stolen password alone isn’t enough.

03Guessed, stolen, captured

Password Attacks

A password is only useful if attackers can’t get it. They have a few favorite ways to try, and the right defense depends on which one you’re facing. Three of these go after the password itself; the fourth steals it as you type.

Brute force

Try every possible combination until one works. Short, simple passwords fall in seconds; long, varied ones can take effectively forever.

Dictionary attack

Skip the random combos and try the obvious ones first — common words and well-known leaked passwords like password123.

Credential stuffing

Take a password leaked from one site and try the same email + password on your other accounts. Works only if you reuse passwords.

Keylogging

Don’t guess at all — use malware that records every key you press and read the password straight off the keyboard. (More in Section 04.)

the defense that beats all four

Long, unique passwords (ideally from a password manager) defeat guessing and stuffing. But the real safety net is multi-factor authentication: even a perfectly stolen or keylogged password isn’t enough, because the attacker still needs the second factor — a code on your phone, a tap, a fingerprint.

04Hostile software

Malware

Malware is any software written to do harm once it’s running on a device — spying, stealing, destroying, or holding things hostage. The types below differ mostly in how they spread and what they do. Read each behavior, guess the name, then flip the card.

“I attach myself to a real file or program. I only spread when you run that infected file — then I infect others.”

flip →
VirusMalicious code attached to a legitimate file; spreads when the file is opened or run.defend: antivirus + don’t open untrusted files

“I don’t need you to run anything. I copy myself across a network all on my own, machine to machine.”

flip →
WormSelf-replicating malware that spreads across networks with no user action needed.defend: keep software patched + firewalls

“I look like something you want — a free game, a handy tool — but I hide a nasty surprise inside.”

flip →
TrojanMalware disguised as legitimate software that you install yourself.defend: only install from trusted sources

“I lock up or encrypt all your files, then demand payment to give them back.”

flip →
RansomwareEncrypts your files and demands a ransom for the key.defend: regular backups — restore instead of paying

“I hide quietly and watch — collecting your activity, data, and habits, and reporting back.”

flip →
SpywareSecretly monitors your activity and sends your data to someone else.defend: anti-malware scans + check app permissions

“I record every key you press, so even a perfect password gets handed straight to my owner.”

flip →
KeyloggerRecords keystrokes to capture passwords and messages as you type.defend: anti-malware + MFA as a backstop
notice the callbacks

Two defenses you’ve already met show up again here. Ransomware is beaten by backups — the same idea as keeping redundant copies so a single failure can’t wipe you out. And a keylogger that steals your password is stopped at the door by MFA from Section 03.

05Caught in the middle

Man-in-the-Middle

In a man-in-the-middle (MITM) attack, someone secretly sits between you and the site you’re trying to reach, quietly relaying your traffic — and reading or changing it on the way. A common trick is a rogue access point (an “evil twin” Wi-Fi network): the attacker sets up a hotspot with a friendly-looking name, you connect, and now everything flows through them.

This is exactly the threat that HTTPS and certificate authorities were built to stop. To impersonate a real site, an attacker would need a valid certificate for that site’s name — and a trusted certificate authority will not issue one to them. Flip the switches and watch what changes.

the takeaway

HTTPS doesn’t stop someone from relaying your traffic — it stops them from reading or faking it, because they can’t produce a certificate a trusted authority vouches for. The lesson: avoid sensitive logins on open Wi-Fi, and never click past a certificate warning. Revisit HTTP vs. HTTPS

06Drowning the line

Denial of Service

A denial-of-service attack doesn’t steal anything — it makes a service unusable by flooding it with more traffic than it can handle, so real users can’t get through. The distributed version (DDoS) sends that flood from thousands of machines at once, usually a botnet of malware-infected devices around the world, which makes it far harder to simply block.

Think of a server’s connection as a pipe with a fixed width — its bandwidth. Junk traffic fills the pipe so legitimate requests get squeezed out. Drag the bots up and watch real users get crowded out; then switch on defenses.

how the Internet ideas fight back

Defenses lean on vocabulary you already know: filtering drops obvious junk before it clogs the pipe, while fault tolerance and redundancy — spreading the load across many servers and adding capacity — let the service absorb a surge instead of toppling. See fault tolerance   See bandwidth

08One-screen review

Defense Recap

The whole page on a single card: what each attack is, and the move that stops it.

attack → defense match the threat to the surface it hits, then to the fix
Phishing
A fake message that tricks you into giving up info. → Slow down, verify the sender, type addresses yourself, turn on MFA.
Password attacks
Brute force, dictionary, credential stuffing, keylogging. → Long unique passwords + multi-factor authentication.
Malware
Hostile software: virus, worm, trojan, ransomware, spyware, keylogger. → Updates, anti-malware, trusted sources, backups.
Man-in-the-middle
An attacker relays your traffic (e.g. a rogue Wi-Fi hotspot). → HTTPS + heed certificate warnings; avoid logins on open Wi-Fi.
Denial of service
A flood of traffic (often a botnet) that crowds out real users. → Filtering + redundancy / extra capacity.
Two defenses everywhere
MFA blunts stolen passwords; backups + redundancy blunt ransomware and outages.
