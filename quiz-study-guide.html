02The big picture

The Internet vs. the Web

The Internet is the global network of networks — the wires, signals, and rules that connect billions of devices. The Web is just one service running on top of it (pages and links delivered over HTTP). Email, video calls, and online games are other services that also ride on the Internet but are not “the Web.” Everything else on this guide is about how data moves across that network safely and quickly.

you should be able to…
  • Explain the difference between the Internet and the Web in one or two sentences.
  • Give an example of an Internet service that is not the Web.
  • Describe what it means for one service to “run on top of” the Internet.

03Names, numbers & rules

Protocols, IP & DNS

Protocols are agreed-upon rules that let different devices understand each other. Every device has an IP address that identifies it (an IPv4 address is four numbers, each one byte). Because numbers are hard to remember, DNS acts like the Internet’s phone book, translating a human-friendly name into the right IP address. Once connected, data is carried by a transport protocol: TCP (reliable, re-sends what’s lost) or UDP (fast, skips re-sending).

you should be able to…
  • Explain what a protocol is and why shared rules matter.
  • Tell whether a given IPv4 address looks valid and say what rule it follows.
  • Explain what DNS does, and identify which part of a name is a subdomain.
  • Contrast TCP and UDP and choose the right one for a situation.

04Built to survive

Packets, Routing & Reliability

Data doesn’t travel as one big chunk. It’s split into packets that each find their own route across the network, can arrive out of order, and get reassembled at the other end. Because there are many possible paths (redundancy), the loss of one router or cable usually doesn’t stop your traffic — that resilience is fault tolerance. Each packet also carries metadata in its header, which can reveal where traffic is going even when the contents are encrypted.

you should be able to…
  • Describe how a message becomes packets and is put back together.
  • Explain fault tolerance and redundancy, and how they relate.
  • Explain why one failed router usually doesn’t break a connection.
  • Explain what packet metadata can reveal even when data is encrypted.
key terms packetheader / metadataroutingfault toleranceredundancyreassembly
explain it

Suppose a router on the path between you and a server fails in the middle of a download. Explain why your download can usually keep going, and which property of the network makes that possible. Dig deeper

05Fast vs. a lot

Speed & Capacity

Two different ideas often get mixed up. Bandwidth (or bit rate) is how much data moves per second — the width of the pipe. Latency is the delay before data starts arriving — how long the round trip takes. A connection can have huge bandwidth and still feel laggy if latency is high. The transmission medium (fiber, copper, radio, satellite) affects both: fiber is fast and low-latency; satellite can carry a lot but has high latency because the signal travels so far.

you should be able to…
  • Define bandwidth, bit rate, and latency, and tell them apart.
  • Describe a situation where latency matters more than bandwidth.
  • Compare transmission media (fiber, copper, radio, satellite) at a high level.

explain it

Describe a real moment when a “fast” connection still felt laggy. Which measure was the real culprit — bandwidth or latency — and how do you know? Dig deeper

06Growth & fairness

Scaling & Access

The Internet keeps growing without anyone being “in charge” because it’s decentralized and built on open protocols — any new network that follows the rules can simply join. But access isn’t equal: the digital divide is the gap between those who have reliable, affordable Internet (and the devices and skills to use it) and those who don’t, with real effects on school, work, and opportunity.

you should be able to…
  • Explain why the Internet can keep growing without a central controller.
  • Connect open protocols and decentralization to scalability.
  • Explain what the digital divide is and give a cause and an effect.
key terms scalabilitydecentralizationopen protocolsdigital divide

07Keeping it private

Encryption & HTTPS

Encryption scrambles data so only the intended party can read it. Symmetric encryption uses one shared secret key — fast, but both sides must already share the key. Asymmetric (public-key) encryption uses a pair: a public key that only locks, and a private key that only unlocks — which solves the problem of sharing keys safely. HTTPS uses both to protect web traffic. Remember: the padlock means the connection is encrypted, not that the site is automatically trustworthy.

you should be able to…
  • Explain symmetric vs. asymmetric encryption and a tradeoff of each.
  • Explain why a public key can be shared with anyone safely.
  • Say what HTTPS protects — and what the padlock does and doesn’t promise.

explain it

Explain why it’s safe to give your public key to anyone — even an attacker — without weakening your security. What can’t they do with it? Dig deeper   On HTTPS

08When it turns hostile

Cybersecurity Threats

Attacks aim at one of two surfaces: people or machines/networks. Phishing tricks a person into giving up information. Password attacks guess, steal, or capture logins. Malware runs hostile software on a device. Man-in-the-middle secretly sits between you and a site. Denial-of-service floods a service so real users can’t get through. Two defenses keep coming back: multi-factor authentication (a stolen password isn’t enough) and backups / redundancy (a single hit can’t wipe you out).

you should be able to…
  • Recognize phishing and explain how it differs from a keylogger.
  • Name the main password attacks and the defense that beats them.
  • Identify the main malware types by what they do.
  • Explain a man-in-the-middle attack and how HTTPS / certificates stop it.
  • Explain a DDoS attack and how filtering and redundancy help.
